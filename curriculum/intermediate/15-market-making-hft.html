Market Making & HFT: The Game Within the Game

Reading time ~15 min • Market Microstructure

HFT and market makers control 50-70% of volume. You can't compete on speed—but you can understand their game and avoid being prey.

How Market Makers Profit

Revenue 1: The Spread

Bid: $100.00, Ask: $100.05, Spread: $0.05

Market maker:
- Buys at $100.00
- Sells at $100.05
- Profit: $0.05/share

On 1M shares/day: $50,000/day = $12.5M/year

Revenue 2: Exchange Rebates

Maker-Taker Model: Exchanges PAY market makers to provide liquidity.

  • Maker (limit order): Receives rebate ($0.0015/share)
  • Taker (market order): Pays fee ($0.0030/share)

On 1M shares/day: $1,500/day = $375,000/year (on top of spread).

Revenue 3: Information Advantage

MMs see order flow in real-time:

  • Large buy orders coming → Widen ask, lift inventory
  • Large sell orders coming → Widen bid, short inventory

They front-run the market (legally, via latency).

HFT Strategies

Strategy 1: Quote Stuffing

Definition: Flooding order book with fake orders to confuse competitors.

11:00:00.001: Post 10,000 orders
11:00:00.050: Cancel 9,800 orders
11:00:00.100: Post 10,000 new orders

Intent: Overload competitors, gain time advantage

Defense: Use limit orders, monitor changes (not static view), trade during high liquidity.

Strategy 2: Latency Arbitrage

Definition: Exploiting speed differences between exchanges.

Price on Exchange A: $100.00
Price on Exchange B: $100.02

HFT: Buy A at $100.00, sell B at $100.02
Profit: $0.02/share, risk-free

Impact on you: Your market order fills at $100.01 (HFT bought first).

Defense: Use IEX (speed bump exchange) or limit orders.

Strategy 3: Order Anticipation

Definition: Predicting large orders before full execution.

Detects: 5,000 buy @ $100, 3,000 @ $100.01, 4,000 @ $100.02

Pattern: Large institutional buyer scaling in

HFT: Buy ahead, sell back at higher price

Defense: Randomize timing, use iceberg orders, scale over days (not minutes).

Why Your Stops Get Hunted

Typical retail: Long AAPL at $150, stop at $149.50 (below support at $149.55).

Market maker sees: Cluster of stops at $149.50 = $10M+ liquidity pool.

Incentive: Push price to $149.50 (costs $10-50K), trigger stops, buy cheap, ride reversal.

It's profitable, so they do it.

Anatomy of a Stop Hunt

12:00: BTC at $45,200
12:05: Support at $45,000, stops at $44,950
12:15: Price tests $45,050
12:16: Aggressive selling → $44,980
12:17: Stops triggered at $44,950
12:18: Price spikes to $44,900 (max carnage)
12:19: REVERSES to $45,300 (stops absorbed)

Signal Pilot Integration: Janus Atlas detects these sweeps. Trade the reversal, not the trap.

Defending Against HFT

Tactic 1: Use Limit Orders

Market orders = guaranteed worst fill. Limit orders = better price (or no fill if setup invalid).

Tactic 2: Avoid Obvious Stop Placement

Bad: Support $100, stop $99.95 (obvious, clustered, hunted)

Better: Support $100, stop $99.50 (structure + ATR buffer, beyond obvious level)

Tactic 3: Trade High Liquidity Hours

HFT works best when thin. Trade 9:30-16:00 ET (tight spreads, harder to manipulate).

Tactic 4: Use IEX (Speed Bump Exchange)

IEX adds 350-microsecond delay to ALL orders, neutralizing HFT latency advantage.

Market Maker Signals (Trade WITH Them)

Signal 1: Spread Widening

When MMs widen spreads without news → Uncertainty, expect move.

Signal 2: Quote Pulling

Large bid wall disappears before hit → Fake wall, price likely breaks down.

Signal 3: Absorption Without Movement

Large volume trades, price doesn't move → MM absorbing (iceberg), strong level.

Practical Strategies

Strategy 1: Trade the Sweep (Not Get Swept)

Setup:

  1. Janus Atlas shows sweep below support
  2. Volume spike + rapid reversal
  3. Enter LONG on reclaim
  4. Stop below swept low

Edge: Trading liquidity event, not being liquidated.

Strategy 2: Avoid First 15 Minutes

Why dangerous: Spread spikes, volatility high, HFT most active.

Better: Wait 9:45-10:00 ET, let volatility settle, enter with Minimal Flow confirmation.

Key Takeaways

  • MMs profit from spread + rebates + information
  • HFT exploits latency, order anticipation, arbitrage
  • Stop hunts are profitable (so they happen)
  • Limit orders > market orders
  • Trade WITH MMs, not against
