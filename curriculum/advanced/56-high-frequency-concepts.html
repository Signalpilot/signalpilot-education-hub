Part 3: How HFTs Actually Make Money

Strategy 1: Market Making (The "Fair" One)

Let's be honest: Not all HFT is evil.

Market makers provide liquidity. They quote both sides of the market and profit from the spread.

Here's how it works:

HFT Market Maker:
Bid: $520.00 (willing to buy 1,000 shares)
Ask: $520.01 (willing to sell 1,000 shares)
Spread: $0.01 per share

If both sides fill:
Buy 1,000 at $520.00 = -$520,000
Sell 1,000 at $520.01 = +$520,010
Profit: $10

Add exchange maker rebate: $0.002/share √ó 2,000 shares = $4
Total profit: $14

Scale: 1 million round-turns per day = $14 million per day

Tiny edge. Massive scale. That's the game.

üéØ Why This Matters to You

Market makers want balanced flow (50% buys, 50% sells).

When flow becomes toxic (informed traders), they widen spreads or pull quotes entirely.

Ever seen bid/ask spread suddenly jump from $0.01 to $0.10? That's HFTs stepping back.

Strategy 2: Latency Arbitrage (The Controversial One)

This is where it gets uncomfortable.

HFTs can see your order coming before it hits the exchange. Here's the play:

Scenario: You're about to buy 10,000 shares of SPY

0ms: HFT detects your order routing to Exchange A
     (SPY bid: $520.00, ask: $520.01)

0.5ms: HFT cancels their $520.01 ask on Exchange A

1ms: HFT buys SPY on Exchange B at $520.01

2ms: Your order arrives at Exchange A
     New ask: $520.03 (HFT's new quote)

Result: You pay $520.03 instead of $520.01
        HFT profit: $0.02 per share √ó 10,000 = $200

From YOUR order. In 2 milliseconds.

Sound familiar? It should. This is basically legal front-running.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's documented.

HFTs argue they provide liquidity. Critics argue they're parasites. The truth? Probably somewhere in between.

Strategy 3: Quote Stuffing (The Shady One)

This is where HFT crosses into manipulation territory.

Quote stuffing: Flood the order book with fake orders to slow competitors and probe for hidden liquidity.

HFT places 10,000 orders in 1 second:

Bid $520.00 (100 shares) ‚Üí Cancel
Bid $520.01 (100 shares) ‚Üí Cancel
Bid $519.99 (100 shares) ‚Üí Cancel
...repeat 10,000 times...

Purpose:
1. Generate noise (slow competitor algorithms)
2. Probe for iceberg orders (detect hidden size)
3. Manipulate NBBO (National Best Bid/Offer)

Result: Order book chaos, flickering prices, retail confusion

You've seen it: Prices jumping all over the place. Rapid quote updates. Order book looking like a video game.

That's not market chaos. That's HFT strategies colliding.

How PFOF Works (And Why You're the Product)

You: Place order to buy 100 shares SPY at market

Your broker (Robinhood):
  1. Receives your order
  2. Sells order to Citadel Securities for $0.002/share
  3. Revenue: 100 √ó $0.002 = $0.20

  1. Receives your order FIRST (before public market)
  2. Fills you at $520.01 (current NBBO ask)
  3. Simultaneously buys on exchange at $520.00
  4. Profit: $0.01/share - $0.002 PFOF = $0.008/share
  5. Total profit: 100 √ó $0.008 = $0.80

You: Get filled at "best price" (technically legal)
    But never had a chance to buy at bid ($520.00)
    Hidden cost: $0.01/share = $1.00

Citadel's annual PFOF profit: $7 billion (2022)
Robinhood's annual PFOF revenue: $1.4 billion (2022)

Tactic 1: Avoid Toxic Times

HFT activity spikes during high-volatility windows. Avoid trading then.

When HFTs Feast

  • 9:30-9:45 AM: Market open chaos
  • 3:45-4:00 PM: Closing auction games
  • News events: FOMC, earnings, economic data
  • Low liquidity: Pre-market, after-hours

Wide spreads, high slippage, HFT paradise.

When You Have an Edge

  • 10:30 AM-12:00 PM: Post-open calm
  • 2:00-3:30 PM: Pre-close stability
  • Tuesday-Thursday: Mid-week flow
  • High liquidity periods: SPY volume > 5M/hour

Tighter spreads, better fills, less HFT dominance.

Tactic 2: Use Limit Orders (Not Market Orders)

Market orders = guaranteed slippage. Limit orders = you control the price.

Bad: Market order to buy SPY
- Pays whatever ask is when order arrives
- HFTs can front-run, reprice
- Slippage: $0.02-0.10 per share

Good: Limit order at bid ($520.00)
- You join the queue
- Wait for natural fill
- Savings: $0.01-0.03 per share
- HFTs can't front-run (you're already there)

Trade-off: You might not get filled immediately. But you save on slippage.

Tactic 3: Hide Your Intent (Iceberg Orders)

Big orders get detected and front-run. Hide your size.

Scenario: Want to buy 10,000 shares SPY

Bad: Single 10,000-share market order
- Visible to HFTs
- Order flow detected
- Front-running begins
- Slippage: $0.05-0.10/share = $500-$1,000 loss

Good: Iceberg order (display 500, hide 9,500)
- HFT sees only 500 shares
- Doesn't trigger detection algorithms
- Reduced slippage: $0.01-0.02/share = $100-200 loss

Most brokers support iceberg orders. Use them for size.

Tactic 4: Trade Longer Timeframes

HFTs dominate sub-second to minutes. They don't compete at hours-to-days.

HFT battleground: 0-60 seconds (scalping)

Your edge: Hours to days (swing trading)
- Fundamentals matter
- Regime matters
- Institutional flow matters
- HFT noise = irrelevant

Takeaway: Trade longer, escape HFT competition

The Bottom Line: How to Profit Alongside (Not Against) HFTs

üéØ Your Anti-HFT Execution Playbook

  1. Use limit orders (never market orders)

  2. Avoid toxic times
    • Never trade 9:30-9:45 AM or 3:45-4:00 PM
    • Avoid FOMC announcements, major news events

  3. Use iceberg orders for size (hide your intent)
    • Display 200-500 shares, hide the rest
    • Prevents HFT algos from detecting large orders
  4. Route to IEX (speed bump exchange)
    • Neutralizes latency arbitrage

  5. Switch to direct-routing broker (avoid PFOF)
    • IBKR, TradeStation, Lightspeed

  6. Trade longer timeframes
    • 4H-Daily charts (HFTs don't compete here)
    • Multi-day holds (capture bigger moves, ignore HFT noise)
  7. Use wide mental stops (not hard stops)
    • Hard stops at round numbers = HFT hunting zones
    • Use mental stops or alerts, potential exit manually if needed
    • Prevents stop hunts during flash crashes
