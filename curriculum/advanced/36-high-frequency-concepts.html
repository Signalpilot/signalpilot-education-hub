High-Frequency Trading Concepts: Latency, Arbitrage & Market Making

Reading time ~14 min • HFT Mechanics

HFT firms make billions on microseconds. You can't compete on speed—but you can understand their strategies and avoid being their liquidity. Learn HFT mechanics, latency arbitrage, and how to trade around their tactics.

What is High-Frequency Trading?

HFT Characteristics

  • Ultra-low latency: 0.5-5 milliseconds (vs. retail 50-200ms)
  • High turnover: Thousands of trades per second
  • Tiny edges: $0.001-0.01 per share (scalable to billions)
  • Market making: Provide liquidity, capture spread
  • Arbitrage: Exploit price differences across venues

Latency & Co-Location

The Speed Arms Race

Latency Breakdown:

Retail Trader (Home Internet):
Decision → Router → ISP → Broker → Exchange
Total: 50-200 milliseconds

HFT Firm (Co-Located):
Decision → Direct fiber → Exchange server (same building)
Total: 0.5-2 milliseconds (100x faster!)

Why Speed Matters

Scenario: SPY bid $520.00, ask $520.01

0ms: Large sell order hits (10,000 shares)
0.5ms: HFT detects order, cancels bid at $520.00
1ms: HFT places new bid at $519.98 (lower)
50ms: Retail order arrives, fills at $519.98

HFT profit: $0.02/share × 10,000 = $200 (from speed)

HFT Market Making

How HFT Market Makers Profit

Bid: $520.00 (HFT buys)
Ask: $520.01 (HFT sells)
Spread: $0.01

If both fill:
Buy 1000 at $520.00 = -$520,000
Sell 1000 at $520.01 = +$520,010
Gross profit: $10

Add maker rebate: $0.002/share × 2000 shares = $4
Total profit: $14 per round-turn

Scale: 1 million round-turns/day = $14M/day

Adverse Selection Risk

HFT's biggest fear: trading with informed traders.

HFT quotes: Bid $520.00, Ask $520.01

Scenario 1: Uninformed flow (retail)
- Random buy/sell (50/50)
- HFT profits from spread (low risk)

Scenario 2: Informed flow (institution about to announce)
- Institution sells aggressively (knows bad news coming)
- HFT buys at $520.00
- News drops, stock falls to $518
- HFT loses $2/share (adverse selection!)

Defense: HFT widens spreads, cancels quotes when detecting toxicity

Arbitrage Strategies

Cross-Exchange Arbitrage

NYSE: SPY bid $520.00, ask $520.01
NASDAQ: SPY bid $520.01, ask $520.02

HFT action:
1. Buy NYSE at $520.01 (take ask)
2. Sell NASDAQ at $520.01 (hit bid)
3. Net: $0.00 (but earns maker rebate on NASDAQ)

Rebate: $0.001/share × 1000 = $1 profit (risk-free!)

Triangular Arbitrage (Crypto)

Exchange A: BTC/USD = $42,000
Exchange B: ETH/USD = $2,200
Exchange C: BTC/ETH = 19.05 (should be 19.09)

Arbitrage:
1. Buy 1 BTC for $42,000 on A
2. Sell 1 BTC for 19.05 ETH on C
3. Sell 19.05 ETH for $41,910 on B

Loss: $90 (spread costs)

If executed in microseconds (before arb closes):
- Potential $50-100 profit per cycle
- Scale to 1000 cycles/day = $50K-100K

Order Anticipation (Front-Running)

How HFTs Detect Large Orders

Signals of incoming institutional order:

1. Dark pool prints (large blocks executing)
2. Iceberg orders (refreshing bid/ask)
3. Momentum shift (unusual buying pressure)
4. Cross-venue activity (orders hitting multiple exchanges)

HFT response:
- Buy ahead of detected institutional buy
- Push price up $0.05-0.10
- Sell to institution at higher price

Quote Stuffing

Flood order book to slow competitors.

HFT places 10,000 orders in 1 second:
- Bid $520.00 (100 shares)
- Cancel
- Bid $520.01 (100 shares)
- Cancel
- Repeat...

Purpose:
1. Generate data noise (slow competitor algos)
2. Probe for hidden orders (icebergs)
3. Manipulate NBBO (best bid/offer)

Result: Order book chaos, retail sees flickering prices

How Retail Can Adapt

Strategy 1: Avoid Toxic Times

High HFT Activity:
- 9:30-9:45 AM (opening chaos)
- 3:45-4:00 PM (closing auction)
- News events (FOMC, earnings)

Low HFT Activity:
- 10:30 AM-12 PM (stable flow)
- Mid-week (Tuesday-Thursday)

Takeaway: Trade during calm windows (better execution)

Strategy 2: Use Limit Orders

Bad: Market order to buy SPY
- Pays ask ($520.01)
- May get front-run (price jumps to $520.05)

Good: Limit order at bid ($520.00)
- Join queue, wait for fill
- Save spread ($0.01/share)
- HFTs can't front-run (you're already in queue)

Strategy 3: Hide Intent (Iceberg Orders)

Want to buy 10,000 shares SPY

Bad: Single market order (signals large size)
- HFT detects, front-runs
- Slippage: $0.05-0.10/share = $500-1000 loss

Good: Iceberg order (display 500, hide 9,500)
- HFT doesn't see full size
- Reduced slippage: $0.01-0.02/share = $100-200 loss

Strategy 4: Use Dark Pools

Large order: Buy 50,000 SPY shares

Lit markets: HFT detects, front-runs (high impact)

Dark pools:
- Hidden liquidity (HFTs can't see)
- Midpoint execution (save spread)
- Lower market impact

Result: Better avg fill ($520.02 vs. $520.10 on lit)

Flash Crashes & Circuit Breakers

2010 Flash Crash

May 6, 2010:
- DOW drops 1,000 points in 5 minutes
- SPY falls 6% in seconds, recovers in minutes

Cause: HFT algos detected large sell order
- Cascading stop-losses triggered
- HFTs withdrew liquidity (widened spreads)
- Algos sold into vacuum (no bids)

Lesson: HFTs provide liquidity in calm, withdraw in chaos

Signal Pilot vs. HFT

Signal Pilot Advantage: Human Discretion

HFT Weakness: Can't interpret context
- News sentiment (bullish vs. bearish headline)
- Chart patterns (support/resistance)
- Multi-timeframe alignment

Signal Pilot + Human:
- Janus sweep (algo detects pattern)
- Human confirms: HTF trend, regime, macro context
- Execute: Better edge than pure HFT (context matters)

Swing Trading > HFT Competition

HFT dominates: Sub-second to minutes (scalping)

Swing trading (hours to days):
- HFTs don't compete (not their timeframe)
- Fundamental edge (macro, regime, flow)
- Lower execution costs (fewer trades)

Takeaway: Trade longer timeframes to avoid HFT battleground

Key Takeaways

  • HFTs trade on microseconds (you can't compete on speed)
  • HFT strategies: market making, arbitrage, front-running
  • Adapt: limit orders, iceberg orders, dark pools
  • Avoid toxic times (9:30-9:45 AM, 3:45-4:00 PM)
  • Trade longer timeframes (escape HFT competition)
