Rule: Track performance over rolling 100-trade window, not recent 5-10 trades. Journal asks: "What's my win rate over last 100 trades?" "What's my avg R over last 100?" This forces her to zoom out from recent results.

Why this works: Removes recency bias. Winning 8 in a row means nothing if your 100-trade win rate is still 62%. Law of large numbers: need 100+ trades to assess true edge. Small samples (last 10 trades) are NOISE, not signal.

Step 5: Absolute Ban on Averaging Down

Rule: If stop loss is hit, position is CLOSED. Cannot add to losing position. Only exception: if original thesis CHANGES due to NEW information (earnings beat, M&A announcement). But if thesis is same and price is lower = exit, don't add.

Why this works: Prevents confirmation bias compounding. Averaging down on same thesis = betting more on a trade that's already wrong. Market is telling you "this trade isn't working." Respect that signal, don't fight it.

Step 6: Daily Loss Limit + Forced Break

Rule: If account loses 2% in one day, STOP trading for 24 hours. Close laptop. No exceptions. After 3 consecutive losing days, STOP trading for 1 week. This prevents revenge trading spiral.

Why this works: Removes emotion from trading. After big loss (like NVDA), emotions are compromised. Desperate to "make it back" = forcing setups that aren't there = more losses. Forced break gives time for emotions to reset. Come back with clear head.

Step 7: Monthly Bias Review (Metacognition)

Part 1: The Cognitive Biases That Destroy Traders

Bias #1: Loss Aversion (Losses Hurt 2Ã— More Than Wins Feel Good)

Definition: Psychological pain of losing $100 > pleasure of winning $100

How it ruins you: You hold losers too long (hoping to break even) and cut winners too fast (lock in profits before they run)

Example:

  • Stock down 10% â†’ "I'll hold until it comes back" (becomes -30%)
  • Stock up 5% â†’ "Better take profits before it reverses" (runs to +40% without you)

Result: Small wins, large losses â†’ account bleeds slowly

ðŸ§  Fix: Automate potential exits via stop-losses and trailing stops. Don't give yourself option to "decide" emotionally. Rules override feelings.

Bias #2: Confirmation Bias (Seeing What You Want to See)

Definition: Seeking information that confirms your existing belief, ignoring contradictory data

How it ruins you: You're bullish â†’ only read bullish news, ignore bearish signals

Example:

  • You buy AAPL at $180 (bullish thesis)
  • AAPL falls to $170 â†’ you search "AAPL bull case" to confirm bias
  • Ignore bearish technicals, credit downgrades, sector rotation OUT of tech
  • AAPL falls to $150 â†’ "I was right to hold!" (survivorship bias)

Fix: Seek DISCONFIRMING evidence. Before entering trade, write down 3 reasons it could FAIL. Force yourself to be devil's advocate.

Bias #3: Recency Bias (Recent Events Feel More Important)

Definition: Overweighting recent outcomes in decision-making

How it ruins you: Win 5 trades in a row â†’ feel invincible â†’ oversize next trade â†’ blow up

Example:

  • Your mean-reversion strategy wins 8 straight trades
  • You assume it ALWAYS works â†’ stop using risk management
  • Next trade hits -20% loss (regime shifted, strategy broken)

Fix: Track statistics over 100+ trades, not last 5. One hot/cold streak means nothing.

Bias #4: Overconfidence (Dunning-Kruger Effect)

Definition: Beginners overestimate skill, experts underestimate luck

How it ruins you: Win first 10 trades â†’ think you're genius â†’ quit day job â†’ market humbles you

The curve:

  • 0-10 trades: Peak overconfidence ("trading is easy!")
  • 10-100 trades: Valley of despair (reality hits, account down)
  • 100-1,000 trades: Competence (realize it's hard, respect risk)
  • 1,000+ trades: Mastery (humble, systematic, profitable)

Fix: Journal every trade. Review monthly. Track when you violated rules due to overconfidence.

Part 2: Emotional States That Sabotage Execution

Emotion #1: Fear (Paralysis & Premature Exits)

Symptoms: Can't pull trigger on setup, potential exit too early, miss best trades

Example: Perfect potential breakout setup forms â†’ you hesitate â†’ price runs +10% without you â†’ FOMO kicks in â†’ you chase at top

Root cause: Previous losses traumatized you (emotional scar tissue)

Fix: Reduce position size until fear disappears. Trade 10 shares instead of 1,000. Rebuild confidence gradually.

Emotion #2: Greed (Overleveraging & Unrealistic Targets)

Symptoms: Risking 10% per trade, holding winners past target (hoping for more), revenge trading losses

Example: Stock up 8% (your target). Greed: "What if it goes to +15%?" â†’ Hold â†’ reverses to -5%

Fix: Set profit targets BEFORE potential entry. Take 50% off at target, let 50% run. Discipline > hope.

Emotion #3: Revenge Trading (Trying to "Get Even")

Trigger: Large loss â†’ emotional â†’ "I need to make it back NOW" â†’ take low-probability setups â†’ lose more

Example:

  • Lose $2,000 on Monday

Part 4: Advanced Psychological Concepts

The Trader's Equation (Van Tharp)

Trading success = (Strategy Ã— Position Sizing Ã— Psychology) - Costs

Breakdown:

  • Strategy: 30% of success (most traders overestimate this)
  • Position sizing: 30% of success (Kelly Criterion, risk management)
  • Psychology: 40% of success (discipline, emotional control)

Implication: Even with mediocre strategy (win rate 50%, R = 1.5), perfect psychology + sizing = profitability. Perfect strategy + bad psychology = bankruptcy.

Expectancy vs Win Rate (Misunderstood)

Myth: "I need 70% win rate to make money"

Reality: Expectancy matters, not win rate

Expectancy = (Win% Ã— Avg Win) - (Loss% Ã— Avg Loss)

Example 1: Low win rate, profitable

  • Win rate: 40%
  • Avg win: $500
  • Avg loss: $100
  • Expectancy = (0.4 Ã— $500) - (0.6 Ã— $100) = $200 - $60 = +$140/trade

Example 2: High win rate, unprofitable

  • Win rate: 70%
  • Avg win: $50
  • Avg loss: $200
  • Expectancy = (0.7 Ã— $50) - (0.3 Ã— $200) = $35 - $60 = -$25/trade

Key insight: Trend traders have 30-40% win rate but HUGE winners (10R, 20R). Mean reversion traders have 65-70% win rate but small winners (1-2R). Both can be profitable if expectancy > 0.
