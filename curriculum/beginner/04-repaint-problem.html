Part 1: What IS Repainting?

The Time-Traveling Indicator

Imagine this: You're watching a chart live. At 2:15 PM, your indicator shows a potential BUY signal at $45,000. Perfect!

You a long position might be considered at $45,010.

At 2:20 PM, the candle closes. You refresh the chart to review your entry area.

The potential BUY signal? It's now at $44,850.

Or worse‚Äîit disappeared completely.

üí° Definition: Repainting

An indicator that changes its past signals or values after the bar closes. What you saw live either moves, disappears, or transforms in hindsight.

It's like time travel‚Äîexcept only the indicator gets to go back. You're stuck with your entry area.

üìñ War Story: The $12k Lesson

Trader discovers "holy grail" indicator: 4.2R expectancy, 3.5 profit factor. Backtested on 2 years of BTC data. Perfection.

Live trading begins: First 3 trades hit stops. "Bad luck," he thinks. Next 5 trades? 1 winner, 4 losers.

2 months later: Down $12,000. Negative expectancy. He reviews his entries in history.

The horror: Every signal he entered? Moved 20-50 points after the bar closed. He was trading signals that never existed.

The indicator was repainting. His backtest? A hallucination. His losses? Very real.

Here's what makes this devastating: Repainting indicators look perfect in backtest because they cheat. They use future information to paint ideal signals in the past.

It's like taking a test where you see the answers afterward, then going back and filling them in. Yeah, you'll score 100%. But you learned nothing.

Part 2: Why Repainting Happens

The Three Ways Indicators Lie

Repainting isn't always intentional (though some scammers do it on purpose). Often, it's coding mistakes. Here are the three main causes:

1. Future Data Leakage

What it is: The indicator pulls data from future bars into the current calculation.

How it happens in code: Using request.security() without lookahead=barmerge.lookahead_off.

Example:

// REPAINTING CODE (bad):
htf_close = request.security(syminfo.tickerid, "D", close)

// This pulls the CURRENT daily close, which hasn't finished yet.
// In backtest, it sees the COMPLETED daily close. Cheating.

Why it's evil: In backtest, it sees finished daily bars. Live? It sees incomplete bars that change every minute.

2. Non-Deterministic Calculations

What it is: The indicator recalculates past values when new data arrives, changing history.

How it happens: Using var variables incorrectly, or loops that reprocess historical bars.

Why it's bad: Your signal at bar 50 changes when bar 100 closes. You can't trust anything.

3. HTF (Higher Timeframe) Repainting

What it is: Using data from a higher timeframe bar that hasn't closed yet.

Example: You're on 15-minute chart. Indicator uses daily data. But the daily bar doesn't close until 5 PM. At 2 PM, that daily bar's values are still changing.

The trap: In backtest, all daily bars are finished. Live? The current daily bar is incomplete.

Part 3: The 4 Detection Methods

How to Catch a Repainting Indicator

You don't need to be a coder to detect repainting. Here are 4 methods‚Äîuse at least 2 for every indicator you trade.

Method 1: Visual Test (Easiest)

Steps:

  1. Open a live chart with your indicator
  2. Screenshot or note where signals appear (specific price + time)
  3. Wait for the bar to close
  4. Refresh your browser
  5. Check if signals moved or disappeared

Verdict: If signals changed = REPAINTING

Repeat 3-5 times to be sure.

Method 2: Replay Test (Most Accurate)

Steps:

  1. Use TradingView's Bar Replay feature
  2. Go back 100+ bars
  3. Play forward bar-by-bar
  4. Mark where signals appear AS THEY APPEAR
  5. Finish replay, compare to historical signals

Verdict: If live signals ‚â† historical signals = REPAINTING

Method 3: Alert Test (Real-World)

Steps:

  1. Set an alert on your indicator's signal
  2. Track 20+ alerts over 1-2 weeks
  3. Record: Alert time + price
  4. Compare alert entries to historical signal locations

Verdict: If alert prices ‚â† signal prices in history = REPAINTING

Method 4: Code Audit (For Coders)

Red flags in Pine Script:

  • request.security() without lookahead=barmerge.lookahead_off
  • security() without [1] offset for HTF data
  • No barstate.isconfirmed check for signals
  • var variables changing past values

Safe code example:

// NON-REPAINTING (good):
htf_close = request.security(syminfo.tickerid, "D", close[1],
             lookahead = barmerge.lookahead_off)

üéì Pro Tip

Use at least 2 methods to audit any indicator. Visual + Alert test is the easiest combo for non-coders.
