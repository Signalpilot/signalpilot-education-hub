Market Maker Algorithms: The Other Side of Your Trade

Every market order you place is filled by a market maker. And they profit from EVERY transaction. Understanding how they operate‚Äîand manipulate‚Äîis the difference between getting fleeced and getting fair fills.

üìñ ~5,100 words ‚è±Ô∏è ~26 min read üìä Intermediate-Bridge
Introduction

Market makers provide liquidity. They also profit from your impatience, your market orders, and your lack of understanding.

When you hit "Buy Market Order," someone is on the other side of that trade: a market maker. Citadel, Virtu, Jane Street, Two Sigma‚Äîthese firms make billions by being the counterparty to retail trades.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Harsh Reality

Citadel Securities made $7.5 billion in revenue in 2022. They don't make money by hoping stocks go up. They make money on every single trade they facilitate‚Äîwhether the market goes up, down, or sideways.

How? Spread capture, adverse selection management, inventory manipulation, and Payment for Order Flow (PFOF).

This lesson breaks down exactly how market makers profit and teaches you to trade in ways that minimize their edge over you.

Part 1: Spread Capture ‚Äî The Core Business Model

Buy the Bid, Sell the Ask, Repeat

The market maker's core profit strategy is spread capture: buy at the bid price, sell at the ask price, pocket the difference.

Example:

  • Bid: $100.00 (the price buyers are willing to pay)
  • Ask: $100.05 (the price sellers are asking)
  • Spread: $0.05
Step-by-Step: How a Market Maker Captures the Spread +

Step 1: Market Maker Posts Quotes

  • Citadel posts bid: $100.00 (willing to buy 10,000 shares)
  • Citadel posts ask: $100.05 (willing to sell 10,000 shares)

Step 2: Retail Trader Sells (Market Order)

  • You place a market sell order for 500 shares
  • You get filled at the bid: $100.00
  • Citadel now owns 500 shares at $100.00

Step 3: Another Retail Trader Buys (Market Order)

  • Another trader places a market buy order for 500 shares
  • They get filled at the ask: $100.05
  • Citadel sells them the 500 shares they just bought from you

Citadel's Profit:

  • Bought from you: 500 shares √ó $100.00 = $50,000
  • Sold to other trader: 500 shares √ó $100.05 = $50,025
  • Profit: $25 (risk-free, in seconds)

Scale: Citadel does this billions of times per day across thousands of tickers. $0.05 per share √ó 10 billion shares = $500 million per day.

Why the Spread Exists

The spread compensates market makers for two risks:

  • Inventory risk: Holding shares for seconds/minutes before finding a counterparty
  • Adverse selection risk: Trading with informed traders who know something the MM doesn't

High Liquidity = Narrow Spreads

Example: AAPL (Apple)

  • Bid: $175.00
  • Ask: $175.01
  • Spread: $0.01 (0.006%)

Why so tight?

  • Millions of shares traded per minute ‚Üí easy to find counterparties
  • Low inventory risk ‚Üí MMs hold shares for milliseconds
  • High competition among MMs ‚Üí they compete on price

MM Profit Strategy: Tiny margins, massive volume. Citadel makes $0.01/share but trades 100 million AAPL shares/day = $1 million/day on AAPL alone.

Low Liquidity = Wide Spreads

Example: Small Cap Stock (Ticker: XYZ)

  • Bid: $10.00
  • Ask: $10.50
  • Spread: $0.50 (5%)

Why so wide?

  • Low volume ‚Üí hard to find counterparties
  • High inventory risk ‚Üí MMs may hold shares for hours/days
  • High adverse selection risk ‚Üí informed traders target illiquid stocks

MM Profit Strategy: Wide margins, lower volume. The spread compensates for risk of being stuck with inventory.

‚ö†Ô∏è Retail Trap: Market Orders on Illiquid Stocks

Scenario: You see a hot penny stock pumped on Reddit. You place a market buy order for 1,000 shares.

Result: You get filled at the ask ($10.50), but the stock's "real" midpoint is $10.25. You just paid 2.4% above fair value.

Lesson: Always use limit orders on illiquid stocks.

Part 2: Adverse Selection ‚Äî The Market Maker's Nightmare

When Smart Money Trades, Market Makers Lose

Adverse selection happens when informed traders‚Äîhedge funds, prop traders, insiders‚Äîtrade with market makers because they have information the MM doesn't.

Example:

  • A hedge fund knows Apple is about to beat earnings (leaked info, proprietary model, etc.)
  • They aggressively buy AAPL at the ask ($175.01) seconds before the announcement
  • Market makers fill the orders (they have no choice‚Äîthey're posting liquidity)
  • Earnings announced: AAPL jumps to $180
  • Market makers are now short AAPL at $175, bleeding losses

‚ö†Ô∏è Why MMs Hate Informed Flow

When a market maker fills an order from an informed trader, they're picking up pennies in front of a steamroller. The spread they earned ($0.01) is wiped out by the adverse move ($5.00).

Solution: MMs use algorithms to detect informed flow and widen spreads or pull liquidity when they sense danger.

How Market Makers Detect Informed Traders

Signal #1: Order Size (Large Orders = Informed) +

Logic: Retail traders buy 10-100 shares. Institutions buy 10,000-100,000 shares.

MM Response: When a 50,000 share market buy order hits, the MM's algo:

  • Widens the spread (ask jumps from $100.05 ‚Üí $100.10)
  • Fills part of the order at higher prices (slippage)
  • Hedges immediately (buys futures to offset risk)

Lesson: Large market orders get terrible fills. Institutions use algorithms (TWAP, VWAP, iceberg orders) to hide their size.

Signal #2: Order Velocity (Rapid Buying = Informed) +

Logic: If buy orders spike suddenly, someone knows something.

Example: Normally, AAPL trades 100,000 shares/minute. Suddenly, 500,000 shares are bought in 10 seconds.

MM Response:

  • Algo detects "toxicity" (high adverse selection probability)
  • Pulls liquidity (removes ask quotes temporarily)
  • Reprices higher when liquidity returns

Lesson: Market makers don't blindly provide liquidity‚Äîthey adjust in real-time based on order flow.

Signal #3: Time of Day (Pre-Market = Informed) +

Logic: Retail traders sleep. If there's heavy trading at 6:00 AM, it's likely institutions reacting to overnight news.

MM Response: Spreads are wider in pre-market and after-hours because:

  • Lower volume ‚Üí harder to hedge
  • Higher adverse selection risk ‚Üí informed traders dominate these hours

Example: AAPL spread during market hours: $0.01. AAPL spread at 7:00 AM: $0.10 (10x wider).

Part 3: Inventory Management ‚Äî Staying Delta-Neutral

Market Makers Don't Want Directional Risk

Market makers are not investors. They don't want to be long or short. They want to be delta-neutral‚Äîmaking money on the spread, not on price movements.

Problem: Order flow is random. Sometimes more buyers than sellers (MM goes short). Sometimes more sellers than buyers (MM goes long).

Solution: Market makers use inventory management algorithms to quickly offload unwanted positions.

How MMs Rebalance Inventory

MM is Long ‚Üí Need to Sell

Scenario: Citadel bought 100,000 AAPL shares from retail sellers. Now they're long 100,000 shares and want to get flat.

Tactic #1: Skew Quotes (Make Selling Attractive)

  • Before: Bid $175.00, Ask $175.05
  • After (skewed): Bid $174.95, Ask $175.00
  • Effect: Ask is now more attractive ‚Üí retail buys ‚Üí Citadel offloads inventory

Tactic #2: Cross with Dark Pools

  • Citadel routes the 100,000 shares to their dark pool
  • Matches with another institution's buy order
  • Offloads inventory instantly, no market impact

Tactic #3: Hedge with Futures

  • Citadel sells SPY futures (or AAPL futures if available)
  • Now delta-neutral: long 100K AAPL shares, short equivalent futures
  • Slowly unwinds both positions over time

MM is Short ‚Üí Need to Buy

Scenario: Citadel sold 100,000 AAPL shares to retail buyers. Now they're short 100,000 shares and need to cover.

Tactic #1: Skew Quotes (Make Buying Attractive)

  • Before: Bid $175.00, Ask $175.05
  • After (skewed): Bid $175.05, Ask $175.10
  • Effect: Bid is now higher ‚Üí retail sells ‚Üí Citadel covers short

Tactic #2: Internalize with Other Retail Orders

  • Citadel waits for retail sell orders to come in
  • Matches them internally (doesn't send to exchange)
  • Covers short position without moving the market

Tactic #3: Buy from Dark Pools

  • Citadel buys 100,000 AAPL shares from a dark pool
  • Covers short instantly, no price impact

üí° Why This Matters to You

When you see the bid/ask move in real-time, it's not random‚Äîit's market makers rebalancing inventory.

Example: You notice AAPL's bid suddenly jumps from $175.00 ‚Üí $175.05. This likely means a market maker is short and needs to buy. If you're a seller, you might get a better fill by waiting 30 seconds for the skew to normalize.

Part 4: Market Maker Manipulation Tactics

The Dark Side of Market Making

Not all market maker tactics are legal or ethical. Some firms use manipulation techniques to maximize profits at retail's expense.

Tactic #1: Spoofing

What it is: Placing large fake orders to create the illusion of supply or demand, then canceling before they execute.

Example: Spoofing to Push Price Down +

Step 1: MM Posts Fake Sell Order

  • Current ask: $100.05 (500 shares)
  • MM posts: 50,000 shares at $100.10

Step 2: Retail Reacts

  • Retail traders see massive sell wall at $100.10
  • They think: "Resistance ahead, price won't go higher"
  • They sell their shares at $100.00

Step 3: MM Cancels Fake Order & Buys

  • MM cancels the 50,000 share sell order (never intended to sell)
  • MM buys the shares retail just sold at $100.00
  • Price recovers to $100.20
  • MM profit: Bought at $100, sells later at $100.20

Legality: Highly illegal (per Dodd-Frank Act), but hard to prove. Regulators must show intent to deceive.

Tactic #2: Layering

What it is: Placing multiple orders on one side of the book to create fake pressure, then trading on the other side.

Example:

  • MM posts 10 fake buy orders at $99.90, $99.85, $99.80 (creating fake support)
  • Retail sees "strong buying interest" and buys at $100.05
  • MM sells to retail at $100.05, then cancels all fake buy orders
  • Result: Retail bought the top, MM sold into strength

‚ö†Ô∏è Real Case: Navinder Singh Sarao (2010 Flash Crash)

Who: Solo trader operating from his parents' house in London

Tactic: Used layering and spoofing to manipulate S&P 500 futures

Impact: Contributed to the May 6, 2010 Flash Crash (Dow fell 1,000 points in minutes)

Punishment: Arrested in 2015, extradited to the US, sentenced to 1 year home confinement

Lesson: If a guy in his basement can cause a flash crash, imagine what well-funded MMs can do.

Tactic #3: Quote Stuffing

What it is: Rapidly placing and canceling thousands of orders to flood the order book and slow down competitors.

Why MMs do it:

  • Creates "noise" that confuses HFT algos from competing firms
  • Slows down data feeds ‚Üí gives the MM's own algos a latency advantage
  • Makes the order book harder to read ‚Üí retail hesitates ‚Üí MM gets better fills

Example: In 2010, studies found that 70% of all market orders were canceled within milliseconds‚Äîmost attributed to quote stuffing.

‚ö†Ô∏è How to Spot Quote Stuffing

Sign #1: Order book shows huge size at multiple levels, but it keeps disappearing

Sign #2: Bid/ask spread is tight, but orders don't execute (phantom liquidity)

Defense: Use limit orders with longer time-in-force (IOC, FOK avoid getting gamed by quote stuffers)

Part 5: Payment for Order Flow (PFOF) ‚Äî How Citadel Wins

Why "Free" Trading Isn't Free

Payment for Order Flow (PFOF) is the practice where brokers (Robinhood, Webull, TD Ameritrade) sell your order to market makers (Citadel, Virtu) instead of routing it to public exchanges.

The Deal:

  • You place a trade on Robinhood (commission-free)
  • Robinhood sells your order to Citadel for $0.002/share
  • Citadel fills your order and pockets the spread

Robinhood's Business Model

Revenue Source: Payment for Order Flow (75% of Robinhood's revenue in 2021)

The Math:

  • Robinhood has 15 million users
  • Each user trades 100 shares/month on average
  • Total: 1.5 billion shares/month
  • Citadel pays Robinhood $0.002/share
  • Robinhood revenue: $3 million/month = $36 million/year

Why Robinhood loves PFOF: They get paid more when you trade more, which is why they gamify trading (confetti animations, push notifications, "free stock" promotions).

Citadel's Business Model

Why Citadel pays for order flow: Retail order flow is uninformed (low adverse selection risk), making it highly profitable.

The Math:

  • Citadel buys 1.5 billion shares/month from Robinhood
  • Average spread captured: $0.01/share (bid-ask spread is $0.02, Citadel captures half)
  • Citadel profit: $15 million/month = $180 million/year
  • Minus payment to Robinhood ($3M/month) = $144M/year net profit

Why retail flow is valuable: Retail traders use market orders, don't hide size, and trade predictably (buy high, sell low). Easy money for MMs.

How You Lose Money

The Hidden Cost: You don't pay commissions, but you pay via price improvement failure.

Example:

  • NBBO (National Best Bid/Offer): Bid $100.00, Ask $100.02
  • You place a market buy order on Robinhood
  • Robinhood routes to Citadel (doesn't send to public exchange)
  • Citadel fills you at $100.02 (the ask)
  • But: On the public exchange, there was a seller willing to sell at $100.01
  • You overpaid by $0.01/share

Scale: If you buy 1,000 shares, you overpaid by $10. Over 100 trades/year = $1,000/year in hidden costs.

‚ö†Ô∏è SEC Study: PFOF Costs Retail $1.5 Billion/Year

Finding: A 2020 SEC study found that retail traders on PFOF brokers (Robinhood, Webull) received worse execution quality than traders on non-PFOF brokers (Fidelity, Vanguard).

Cost: On average, $0.01-$0.02 worse per share‚Äîtotaling $1.5 billion/year across all retail traders.

Lesson: "Free" trading isn't free. You pay via worse fills.

Latency Arbitrage via PFOF

The most insidious aspect of PFOF: market makers see your order before it hits the exchange, giving them a latency advantage to front-run you.

Example: How Citadel Front-Runs Your Order +

Step 1: You Place a Market Buy Order

  • You buy 1,000 shares of AAPL at market on Robinhood
  • Current NBBO: Bid $175.00, Ask $175.02

Step 2: Robinhood Sends Order to Citadel

  • Robinhood doesn't route to NYSE‚Äîroutes to Citadel instead
  • Citadel receives your order 2 milliseconds before public exchanges

Step 3: Citadel Front-Runs

  • Citadel's algo sees your 1,000 share buy coming
  • Citadel buys 1,000 AAPL shares on NYSE at $175.00 (the bid)
  • Citadel then sells to you at $175.02 (the ask)
  • Citadel profit: $20 risk-free

Why it's legal: Citadel claims they "provided liquidity" and "improved" your price (you paid the NBBO ask, not worse). But they knew your order was coming, which is a form of information asymmetry.

Part 6: How to Trade Against Market Makers

Defense Strategies for Retail Traders

You can't eliminate market maker edge, but you can minimize it. Here's how professionals trade to avoid getting fleeced.

Strategy #1: Always Use Limit Orders

Rule: Never use market orders unless execution speed matters more than price.

Why: Limit orders let you set the price, not the market maker.

Example:

  • NBBO: Bid $100.00, Ask $100.05
  • Bad: Market buy ‚Üí filled at $100.05
  • Good: Limit buy at $100.02 ‚Üí wait for seller, save $0.03/share

üí° Pro Tip: Use Midpoint Limit Orders

Tactic: Place limit orders at the midpoint of the bid-ask spread.

Example: Bid $100.00, Ask $100.04 ‚Üí Midpoint = $100.02. Place limit buy at $100.02.

Benefit: You split the spread with the MM instead of giving them the entire edge.

Strategy #2: Trade During Liquid Hours

Rule: Trade between 10:00 AM - 3:00 PM ET (avoid first/last hour and after-hours).

Why:

  • Spreads are tightest during peak liquidity hours
  • More competition among MMs ‚Üí better fills
  • Lower adverse selection risk ‚Üí MMs don't widen spreads as much

Example:

  • AAPL spread at 10:00 AM: $0.01
  • AAPL spread at 9:31 AM (market open): $0.05 (5x wider)
  • AAPL spread at 6:00 PM (after-hours): $0.15 (15x wider)

Strategy #3: Avoid PFOF Brokers (Or Route to IEX)

Rule: Use brokers that don't sell order flow (Fidelity, Vanguard, Interactive Brokers).

Why: PFOF brokers route to Citadel, giving MMs a latency advantage. Non-PFOF brokers route to public exchanges where you get price priority.

Alternative: If stuck with Robinhood, route orders to IEX (Investors Exchange)‚Äîa public exchange designed to eliminate latency arbitrage.

How to Route to IEX on Interactive Brokers +

Step 1: Open order entry ticket

Step 2: Click "Route" dropdown

Step 3: Select "IEX" (Investors Exchange)

Step 4: Place limit order

Benefit: IEX has a "speed bump" (350 microseconds delay) that prevents HFTs and market makers from front-running you.

Strategy #4: Don't Chase‚ÄîLet the Market Come to You

Rule: If your limit order doesn't fill immediately, don't chase by raising your limit price.

Why: Market makers want you to chase. They intentionally pull liquidity to force you to pay more.

Example:

  • You place limit buy at $100.02
  • Ask jumps to $100.10 (MM pulled liquidity)
  • Bad move: Cancel and rebuy at $100.10 (you just got played)
  • Good move: Wait 30 seconds. Ask will likely return to $100.05 once MM realizes you won't chase.
Quiz: Test Your Understanding

üéÆ Market Maker Mastery Check

1. AAPL bid/ask is $175.00 / $175.05. You place a market sell order for 500 shares. What price do you get?

A) $175.00 (the bid)
B) $175.05 (the ask)
C) $175.025 (the midpoint)

Correct! Market sell orders are filled at the bid price ($175.00). Market makers buy from you at the bid and sell to others at the ask, capturing the spread.

Lesson: Market orders always give the market maker the edge. Use limit orders to avoid this.

2. You see a huge sell order (50,000 shares) appear at $100.10, but it disappears before anyone can fill it. What just happened?

A) A large institution changed their mind
B) Spoofing‚Äîa market maker posted a fake order to manipulate price
C) High-frequency trader testing liquidity

Correct! This is spoofing‚Äîplacing large fake orders to create the illusion of supply/demand, then canceling before execution.

Why MMs do it: The fake sell wall at $100.10 scares retail into selling at $100.00. The MM then buys at $100.00 and cancels the fake order.

Legality: Highly illegal, but hard to prove. The MM can claim they "changed their mind."

3. You trade on Robinhood (PFOF broker). NBBO is $100.00 / $100.02. You place a market buy. Citadel fills you at $100.02. Did you get a fair price?

A) Yes‚Äîyou paid the NBBO ask, so it's fair
B) No‚Äîthere might have been a better price on a public exchange
C) Yes‚ÄîRobinhood is commission-free, so you saved money

Correct! Even though you paid the NBBO ask ($100.02), there might have been sellers at $100.01 on NYSE or Nasdaq. But Robinhood routed to Citadel instead of the exchange, so you didn't get price priority.

Hidden cost: Over hundreds of trades, these $0.01 overages add up to hundreds or thousands of dollars per year.

Defense: Use non-PFOF brokers (Fidelity, Interactive Brokers) or route to IEX.

Key Takeaways

üí° Understanding Market Makers = Better Fills

  • Spread capture is the MM's core profit model: buy bid, sell ask, repeat billions of times.
  • Adverse selection = informed traders cost MMs money, so MMs widen spreads when they detect "toxic" flow.
  • Inventory management: MMs skew quotes, use dark pools, and hedge with futures to stay delta-neutral.
  • Spoofing, layering, quote stuffing = illegal-but-common manipulation tactics MMs use.
  • PFOF = "free" trading isn't free. You pay via worse fills and latency arbitrage.
  • Defense: Use limit orders, trade during liquid hours, avoid PFOF brokers, route to IEX.
