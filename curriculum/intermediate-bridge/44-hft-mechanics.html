Part 1: What Is HFT?

Definition & Scale

Volume: 50-70% of US equity volume (varies by market conditions)

Profit per trade: Fractions of a penny, scaled across millions of trades

Part 3: How HFTs Exploit Retail

Tactic #1: Order Anticipation

How it works:

  1. HFT detects large institutional order (10,000 shares buying AAPL at multiple venues)
  2. HFT buys AAPL ahead of institution
  3. Institution's continued buying pushes price up
  4. HFT sells to institution at higher price

Profit: $0.01-0.05/share Ã— 10,000 shares = $100-500 (risk-free, using speed advantage)

Tactic #2: Latency Arbitrage (Cross-Venue)

Setup: Stock trades on 13+ exchanges (NYSE, Nasdaq, BATS, IEX, dark pools, etc.)

Exploit: Price updates don't arrive simultaneously across all venues

Example:

  • AAPL trades at $150.00 on all exchanges
  • Large sell order hits NYSE â†’ price drops to $149.98
  • HFT sees NYSE update in 0.1ms via colocation
  • Other exchanges still show $150.00 (their systems update in 1ms)
  • HFT sells AAPL at $150.00 on Nasdaq (before price updates)
  • HFT buys AAPL at $149.98 on NYSE
  • Profit: $0.02/share risk-free

Tactic #3: Quote Stuffing

What: Flood market with fake orders, cancel before execution

Why: Slow down competitor algos (create processing delays)

Scale: HFTs can place 10,000 orders/second, cancel 99.9%

Impact on retail: Order book looks messy, spreads widen, worse fills

Tactic #4: Maker-Taker Fee Arbitrage

How exchanges work: Makers (provide liquidity) get rebate ($0.0020/share), takers (remove liquidity) pay fee ($0.0030/share)

HFT strategy:

  1. Post resting bid at $100.00 on exchange A (maker rebate = $0.0020/share)
  2. Get filled by retail market order
  3. Immediately sell at $100.00 on exchange B (taker fee = $0.0030/share)
  4. Net profit: $0.0020 rebate - $0.0030 fee + spread capture = profit

Part 4: HFT Red Flags & Defense

Signs HFTs Are Active

  • Spoofing: Large orders appearing/disappearing rapidly
  • Spread widening: During volatile periods, HFTs pull liquidity (spreads gap)
  • Odd lot prints: Lots of 17 shares, 83 shares (HFT inventory management)
  • Microsecond timestamps: Trades executed 0.001s apart (impossible for humans)

How to Defend Against HFTs

Defense #1: Use Limit Orders Only

Why market orders are dangerous: HFTs see your market order, move the price against you before execution

Better approach: Use limit orders at or near current price

Example: Stock bid $100.00, ask $100.05. Don't market buy (you'll get $100.10 after HFT front-runs). Instead, limit buy at $100.03 (control your fill).

Defense #2: Route Through IEX

IEX innovation: 350-microsecond speed bump (38 miles of coiled fiber)

Effect: All orders delayed equally â†’ HFT latency advantage neutralized

How to use: Most brokers allow routing preference (select "IEX" in order settings)

Defense #3: Trade During Liquid Hours Only

HFT thrives in: Pre-market, after-hours, lunch hour (low volume, wide spreads)

Best trading hours: 9:30-11:30 AM, 2:00-4:00 PM ET (peak institutional volume)

Avoid: 12:00-1:30 PM (lunch), 4:00-8:00 PM (after-hours)
