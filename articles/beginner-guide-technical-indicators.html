A Beginner’s Guide to Technical Indicators

Reading time ~10 min • Educational only

Working theory: indicators are thermometers, not doctors. They measure something about price/volume; they don’t predict the future. The edge is in process—choosing a few tools that answer specific questions and combining them without double‑counting the same thing.

What indicators actually measure

Group them by the question they answer:

  • Trend / regime: moving averages (EMA50/100/200), slope, MACD. “Is price generally drifting up, down, or sideways?”
  • Momentum / impulse: RSI, Stoch, MACD histogram. “How fast is it moving right now?”
  • Volatility: ATR, Bollinger Bands. “How loud is the market?”
  • Participation / flow: OBV, CVD, RVOL/PVA. “Is there real money behind the move?”
  • Structure / context: higher‑highs/lows, swings, supply/demand, anchored VWAP/levels.

Where people go wrong

  • Redundancy: three oscillators ≠ three confirmations. They’re often correlated.
  • Role confusion: using RSI to decide trend, or a moving average crossover to time entries to the second.
  • Repainting traps: anything that changes after the bar closes will lie to you in backtests.

A minimal, sane stack

Bias: 200‑EMA slope or MACD+ regime • Participation: PVA/OBV confirmation • Structure: SD zones & anchors • Timing: RSI Triad / SRSI+.

This keeps each tool in its lane. You can trade any market and timeframe with that skeleton.

Starter checklist

  • Decide bias first. If regime is unclear, skip.
  • Require participation. No flow → no trade.
  • Place trades at structure, not in the void.
  • Use a fixed stop and size to risk ≤1R.

Practice: the Trend‑Dip play

  1. Bias Up (MACD+ or EMA stack).
  2. PVA prints Bull or EVR Climax through anchor.
  3. Pullback into demand/VWAP area.
  4. RSI Triad turns up; enter with stop below structure.
Reminder: Educational only. These are methods to practice, not P&L promises.
